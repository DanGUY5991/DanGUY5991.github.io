Sensemaking & The ESR Cycle

What is Sensemaking?

Sensemaking is the process of asking “what is going on?” in messy or uncertain situations and building a shared story that feels “good enough” to guide action. [1]

At work, this happens when teams try to understand a sudden policy change, a new technology, or a crisis and talk it through until they agree on how to respond. [2]

Weick argues that sensemaking is grounded in identity, looks backward (retrospective), is social, and aims for plausibility rather than accuracy.

The ESR Cycle: Enactment, Selection, Retention

Weick’s model breaks sensemaking into three linked steps. [3]

1

Enactment

Act to Create Meaning: People do something to "create" the environment they face. By acting, they bring certain parts of the chaotic world into focus.

Example: Staff testing new AI tools without clear rules, effectively defining how they will be used.

2

Selection

Interpret & Filter: Teams look back at what happened, pick cues, and agree on a story that reduces confusion.

Example: Debriefing a failed project and agreeing on "why" it failed to guide future choices.

3

Retention

Store & Remember: The explanation is stored in memory or policy, becoming "how we do things here."

Example: A new checklist created from the debrief that shapes how the next project is run.

Why It Matters

For Leaders

Understanding ESR helps leaders see that people aren't just following rules—they are actively creating meaning. Leaders can support this by encouraging open discussion and being careful about which "stories" become policy. [1]

For Employees

Noticing these steps helps staff question unhelpful habits and propose new ways of understanding issues, improving adaptability. [4]
