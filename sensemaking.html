Sensemaking & The ESR Cycle

What is Sensemaking?

Sensemaking is the process of asking “what is going on?” in messy or uncertain situations and building a shared story that feels “good enough” to guide action. [1]

At work, this happens when teams try to understand a sudden policy change, a new technology, or a crisis and talk it through until they agree on how to respond. [2]

Weick argues that sensemaking is grounded in identity, looks backward (retrospective), is social, and aims for plausibility rather than accuracy.

The ESR Cycle: Enactment, Selection, Retention

Weick’s model breaks sensemaking into three linked steps. [3]

1

Enactment

Act to Create Meaning: People do something to "create" the environment they face. By acting, they bring certain parts of the chaotic world into focus.

Example: Staff testing new AI tools without clear rules, effectively defining how they will be used.

2

Selection

Interpret & Filter: Teams look back at what happened, pick cues, and agree on a story that reduces confusion.

Example: Debriefing a failed project and agreeing on "why" it failed to guide future choices.

3

Retention

Store & Remember: The explanation is stored in memory or policy, becoming "how we do things here."

Example: A new checklist created from the debrief that shapes how the next project is run.

Why It Matters

For Leaders

Understanding ESR helps leaders see that people aren't just following rules—they are actively creating meaning. Leaders can support this by encouraging open discussion and being careful about which "stories" become policy. [1]

For Employees

Noticing these steps helps staff question unhelpful habits and propose new ways of understanding issues, improving adaptability. [4]

Weick’s 7 Properties of Sensemaking

Sensemaking has seven main properties that show up clearly in everyday workplace life. [5]

1. Grounded in Identity

"Who are we?" Sensemaking starts from self-concept. How a person understands their role shapes what they notice and how they interpret events. [6]

The Crisis Response "When the system crashed, Alex didn't wait for instructions. Seeing herself as a 'problem-solver' rather than just an analyst, she immediately started diagnosing the root cause while others waited for approval."

Example: A "problem-solver" sees a crisis as a challenge; a "subordinate" sees it as a threat.

2. Retrospective

"What just happened?" People make sense by looking back. We frame events after they occur to understand what they mean. [5]

The Project Post-Mortem "The launch wasn't perfect, but the team's real understanding came during the debrief. By re-telling the story of the past two weeks, they agreed that the 'glitch' was actually a feature conflict, not a server error."

Example: Post-mortems where teams decide why a project failed and what it "means" for the future.

3. Enactive of Environments

"We create our reality." People don’t just react; their actions help create the situation they interpret. [6]

The Spreadsheet Strategy "Faced with a vague corporate strategy, the marketing team built their own tracking spreadsheet. Six months later, that spreadsheet became the official company reporting tool—their action defined the new reality."

Example: Staff building their own spreadsheets in response to vague strategy, effectively "enacting" the new process.

4. Social

"It happens in conversation." Sensemaking is a social process of talking, sharing, and debating interpretations. [7]

The Coffee Break Consensus "After the confusing town hall, nobody asked questions. But in the 10 minutes by the coffee machine afterwards, three managers compared notes and decided what the CEO 'really meant' by 'efficiency,' aligning their teams based on that chat."

Example: "What did the boss really mean?" chats that form a shared team understanding.

5. Ongoing

"It never stops." Understanding is always updating as new things happen. It is a continuous flow, not a one-time event. [5]

The Merger Rumors "First it was just a rumor of a merger, then an announcement of 'partnership,' then 'integration.' With every new email and hallway whisper, the team's understanding of their job security shifted day by day."

Example: How views on a merger shift slowly over months of rumors and announcements.

6. Focused on Extracted Cues

"Small details matter." In complexity, we focus on small cues (tone, a single metric) and use them to build a larger story. [6]

The Silence "The VP didn't say 'no,' but she didn't smile when the budget was mentioned. That split-second reaction was the cue the director used to tell his team: 'Budget cuts are coming, prepare for a freeze.'"

Example: Inferring a major pivot from a single line in an executive email.

7. Driven by Plausibility

"Good enough to move on." We need a story that is reasonable and actionable, not necessarily 100% accurate. [7]

The 'Good Enough' Fix "We didn't know *exactly* why customers were leaving, but 'pricing' seemed like the most plausible reason. The team rallied around a new discount strategy—not because they had perfect data, but because it gave them a clear way to act."

Example: Acting on a "plausible" reason for customer churn to define a strategy, even without perfect data.
